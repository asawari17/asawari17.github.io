Hello! I am a third year PhD candidate in physical chemistry at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill with the Lu group. My research focuses on understanding the physics of information transduction via biological sensors that perceive a cell's environment and transmit the information to its interior leading to decisions that are essential for the cell's survival. These "molecular machines" transmit the environmental information by undergoing configurational changes that stimulate downstream reations. Since these sensors form a bottleneck between the environment and the cells interior, the temporal trajectories of their configurationational states contains all the necessary information about the environment. The projects I have worked on so far have been directed towards uncovering the limits of information content in the stochastic trajectories of sensors.

Under this general topic, I have worked on multiple projects that have focused on: (1) Mutliplexing in biological receptors, (2) General theory of distinguishability of Markovian trajectories and (3) Transient performance of sensors under concentration change in environment.

Previously, as an undergrad I worked on various research projects in theoretical and computational chemistry. You can find more details about my research in my published papers.

Teaching has also been an intergral part of my PhD journey and I have taught multiple chemistry lab courses.

In my free time I enjoy solving puzzles, traveling, cooking and looking after my plants!
