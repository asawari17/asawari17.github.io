Hello! I am a fourth year PhD candidate in chemistry at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill with the Lu group. I am interested in uncovering the role of temporal dynamics behind allosteric signal transduction in ligand-receptor sensors. These cell surface proteins are important drug targets and understanding their role in sensing paves a way to better understand the mechanism of drug action and efficacy. My research has involved modeling and simulations to study these systems. I have also done courses that have helped me develop skills in the general area of computer aided drug discovery (CADD) as well as machine learning with graphs, I am passionate to pursue a career in quantitative systems pharmacology research and contribute in better and quicker medicines to human kind. The projects I have worked on so far have been directed towards uncovering the limits of information content in the stochastic trajectories of sensors -- these results could help understand disease biology as well as protein functions better.

Under this general topic, I have worked on multiple projects that have focused on: (1) Mutliplexing in biological receptors, (2) General theory of distinguishability of Markovian trajectories and (3) Transient performance -- withdrawal effect in sensors under concentration change in environment.

Previously, as an undergrad I worked on various research projects in theoretical and computational chemistry as a summer intern as well as for my bachelor's and master's theses with the Cherayil group and the Errington group at the Indian Institue of Science, Bangalore and SUNY Buffalo respectively. You can find more details about my research in my published papers.

Teaching has also been an intergral part of my PhD journey and I have taught multiple chemistry lab courses.
